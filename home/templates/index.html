Background

Wildlife crime alone is estimated to be worth $15 to $20 billion annually. It is recognized as the fourth largest global illegal trade preceded only by narcotics, human trafficking and weapons trade. Conservation of biodiversity is key to ecological sustainability and long-term growth. The world today is threatened by an impending mass extinction brought upon by unchecked growth, corporate greed, complicity and negligence coupled with burgeoning expansion of human population. With each passing year, the growing demand for wood based products keep on increasing. The forests and their ecosystems are continuously encroached upon to feed the economic systems that have seen exponential growth in the last century. The vast expanse of these forests are in remote areas, removed from direct surveillance. The limits of the law enforcement agencies, especially in developing countries where these endangered flora and fauna are found, are stretched in its effort to enforce local and international laws enacted for preservation. What complicates matters is that these vulnerable species are economically valuable leading to unfair trade practices. This is true in countries where the governance structure is not capable or willing to enforce the rules required for global conservation efforts. Illegal timber trade ranks among the top categories in illegal trade, prevention of which is important to national security among other aspects. Such trade adversely impacts biodiversity and increases extinction risk for commercially valuable timber species. There has been significant efforts to curb deforestation, but of late reports suggest the success of these measures have been plateauing. Deforestation in critical parts of South America, Africa and Asia have been monitored, yet the practice of illegal trade remains persistent. Thus it is imperative to take more innovative approaches to detect and track illegal trade patterns, which would lead to a more targeted approach in curbing this problem.

Proposed Framework

An integrated framework to tackle the challenge of detecting illegal timber trade through systematically highlighting patterns of illegal timber trade Our system is merges disparate data from a multitude of sources to gather targeted domain knowledge on timber trade. We apply Machine Learning methods on trade data records along with domain knowledge to highlight suspicous trade instances in an interactive interface that allows domain experts to track, analyze and understand illegal timber trade better.
